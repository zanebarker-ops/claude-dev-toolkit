# UX / HCD Vocabulary Reference

**A/B Testing** — Comparing two versions of a design to see which performs better with users.

**Accessibility (a11y)** — Designing products usable by people with disabilities (visual, motor, cognitive, auditory).

**Affinity Diagram** — Organizing research findings into groups based on natural relationships.

**Affordance** — A property of an object that suggests how it can be used (e.g., a button looks clickable).

**Atomic Design** — Design methodology using atoms, molecules, organisms, templates, and pages.

**Card Sorting** — Research method where users organize topics into categories to inform information architecture.

**Cognitive Load** — The mental effort required to use a product. Lower is better.

**Contextual Inquiry** — Observing and interviewing users in their natural environment while they work.

**Dark Pattern** — A design intentionally crafted to trick users into unintended actions.

**Design Sprint** — A 5-day process for answering critical business questions through design and prototyping.

**Design System** — A collection of reusable components, guided by clear standards, that can be assembled to build applications.

**Design Thinking** — A human-centered approach to innovation: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test.

**Double Diamond** — A design process model with four phases: Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver.

**Empathy Map** — A collaborative tool to articulate what we know about a user (says, thinks, does, feels).

**Error State** — The UI shown when something goes wrong. Good error states explain the problem and suggest a fix.

**Fitts's Law** — The time to reach a target is a function of distance and size. Make important targets large and close.

**Gestalt Principles** — Laws of visual perception (proximity, similarity, closure, continuity, figure-ground).

**Heuristic Evaluation** — Usability inspection method where experts evaluate against established principles.

**Human-Centered Design (HCD)** — An approach that prioritizes understanding human needs, capabilities, and behaviors throughout the design process.

**Information Architecture (IA)** — The structural design of shared information environments.

**Interaction Design (IxD)** — Designing interactive products to support the way people communicate and interact.

**Journey Map** — A visualization of the process a user goes through to accomplish a goal, including emotions and pain points.

**Lean UX** — Applying lean principles to UX: build-measure-learn cycles with minimum viable designs.

**Mental Model** — A user's internal representation of how something works. Designs should match users' mental models.

**Microinteraction** — Small, contained product moments that accomplish a single task (toggle, like, pull-to-refresh).

**Minimum Viable Product (MVP)** — The simplest version of a product that delivers core value to early users.

**Modal** — A dialog that requires user interaction before returning to the main interface. Use sparingly.

**Persona** — A fictional character representing a user segment, based on research data.

**Progressive Disclosure** — Showing only essential information first, with details available on demand.

**Prototype** — An early model of a product used for testing. Can range from paper sketches to interactive code.

**Responsive Design** — Design that adapts to different screen sizes and orientations.

**Service Design** — Designing the end-to-end experience of a service, including all touchpoints and backstage processes.

**Signifier** — A perceivable indicator of what actions are possible (e.g., underlined text = link).

**Storyboard** — A sequence of illustrations showing how a user interacts with a product in context.

**System Usability Scale (SUS)** — A 10-question standardized questionnaire for measuring perceived usability.

**Task Analysis** — Breaking down how users accomplish goals, step by step.

**Tree Testing** — Evaluating the findability of topics in a website's information architecture.

**Usability** — The extent to which a product can be used to achieve goals with effectiveness, efficiency, and satisfaction.

**User Flow** — A diagram showing the path a user takes through a product to complete a task.

**User Research** — Systematic study of target users to inform design decisions.

**User Story** — A short description of a feature from the user's perspective: "As a [user], I want [goal] so that [reason]."

**Wireframe** — A low-fidelity layout showing the structure and content of a page without visual design.
